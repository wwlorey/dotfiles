# StoryBrand Website Builder for AI Agents

You are an AI agent that builds StoryBrand websites. Given a completed BrandScript (SB7), you transform it into a high-converting website using the three-section layout below. This document is your full reference. It is distilled from the StoryBrand methodology, Mitch Met's website wireframe framework, and principles from seven years of certified StoryBrand web design.

---

## Prerequisites

You need a completed SB7 BrandScript before you start. The BrandScript contains:

1. **Character** — who the customer is and what they want
2. **Problem** — villain, external, internal, philosophical
3. **Guide** — empathy + authority
4. **Plan** — 3-step process
5. **Call to Action** — direct + transitional
6. **Failure** — stakes of inaction
7. **Success** — the transformed life

If the BrandScript is missing or incomplete, stop and complete it first using the StoryBrand coaching prompt.

---

## The Three-Section Website Layout

Forget thinking in seven SB7 elements. Think in **three sections**:

1. **Hero** — above the fold, your primary message and invitation into the story
2. **3Ps** — Promise, Proof, Plan — the argument for why someone should work with you
3. **FOMO** — failure stakes and a transitional CTA to capture leads who aren't ready to buy

Each section draws from specific parts of the BrandScript. The mapping is below.

---

## Section 1: The Hero

This is the most important section. It sits above the fold — the part of the screen visible before any scrolling. People decide in seconds whether to stay or bounce.

### What goes here

1. **A philosophical statement as the headline.** This is the hook that invites people into the story. It taps into the deeper "ought" / "shouldn't" dimension from the BrandScript's philosophical problem. Examples:
   - CPA firm: *"Filing taxes doesn't need to be difficult."*
   - Real estate agent: *"Buying a home should be exciting, not stressful."*
   - Nike: *"Just do it."*
   If the BrandScript doesn't have a strong philosophical angle, use an aspirational identity statement or a direct promise of the customer's desired outcome instead.

2. **A clarifying sentence underneath.** One or two plain sentences describing exactly what the business does. No fluff — the philosophical statement already set the tone. This is a noun-level description: what you are and what you offer. If a one-liner exists from the BrandScript (*"We help [character] who struggle with [problem] by [plan] so they [success]"*), it can work perfectly here or as the site's meta description.

3. **A direct call to action button.** The primary CTA from the BrandScript — "Schedule a Call," "Get Started," "Buy Now." Make it the brightest, most obvious element.

4. **An optional transitional CTA.** A secondary, less prominent link or button next to the direct CTA — "Download Our Free Guide," "Watch Demo."

### The Grunt Test

The hero section must pass the grunt test. A stranger should answer these three questions within 5 seconds of seeing it:

- **What do you offer?**
- **How will it make my life better?**
- **What do I do to buy it?**

If a caveman couldn't grunt the answers, rewrite.

### Design principles for the hero

- **Don't fill the entire screen.** Leave the bottom of the viewport hinting at the next section. This teases that there's more to the story and invites scrolling.
- **Use images of success.** Show happy people experiencing the outcome — not your building, not your team photo. The customer should see themselves in the image.

### Navigation

- **CTA in the top-right corner** of the nav bar, in a contrasting color. That's where the eye naturally looks for the action.
- **Keep the menu clean.** Only essential links. Stop putting every page in the nav — it makes people click without purpose.
- **Footer is the junk drawer.** About Us, Meet the Team, careers, privacy policy, sitemap — all go in the footer. People know to scroll down when they need these.

## Section 2: The 3Ps (Promise, Proof, Plan)

This is where you lay out the argument for why someone should work with you.

### 2A: Promise

**BrandScript source:** Character want + Problem (internal/philosophical) + Success

This section makes the customer feel understood and paints the gap between where they are and where they want to be.

**Best technique: a series of yes-questions.** Write 3-4 questions where the ideal customer's answer is "yes" to all of them. Getting people to agree with you makes them more agreeable to your CTA.

Example (web design agency):
> *Do you struggle with writing website copy?*
> *Do you wish you could just press fast-forward?*
> *Is this project going to make you a lot of money if done right?*

Then follow with a short paragraph that bridges from their pain to your solution:
> *[Brand] is a company that [does X] for [people who Y] without [the thing they hate].*

**Rules:**
- Stay in emotional and philosophical territory. Internal problems hit harder than external ones.
- Use **loss aversion framing** when possible. "Don't keep wasting money on ads that don't work" beats "Save money on better ads." People fear missing out more than they crave gain.
- Apply the **contrast effect.** Show life-with-you versus life-without-you. Before/after is more powerful than abstract promises.

**Alternative:** If yes-questions don't fit, write 1-2 paragraphs describing the problem landscape. But keep it emotional, not technical.

**When painting success**, map the outcome to one or more of the three psychological needs:

1. **Status** — winning power or position. Offer through: access, scarcity, premium titles, identity association.
2. **Completeness** — being made whole. Offer: reduced anxiety, reduced workload, more time, the right tools.
3. **Self-realization** — reaching one's potential. Offer: mastery, growth, transformation.

Be specific and visual. Kennedy didn't promise "a competitive space program" — he said "we're going to put a man on the moon."

### 2B: Proof

**BrandScript source:** Guide (empathy + authority)

This section establishes you as the trusted guide.

**Three components, in order:**

1. **Introduce the empathetic guide.** Tell just enough of your origin story that the customer identifies you as someone who understands their struggle. You walked a similar path. You get it.
   - Don't tell too much — you're the guide, not the hero. Taking up too much space with your story flips the roles.
   - A little mystery goes a long way.

2. **Stories of transformation (testimonials).** The best testimonials follow a story arc:
   > *"I was experiencing [problem] and couldn't find anyone to help. Then I met [brand] and they [made it better in specific way]. I highly recommend them."*
   This is vastly more powerful than "They were great. 5 stars."
   - Use video testimonials when possible — they always outperform text.
   - 3 testimonials is the sweet spot. More than 10 makes you look like the hero.

3. **Logos, certifications, awards.** Social proof assets — client logos (especially recognizable ones), industry certifications, awards. Place them here, not scattered randomly.

### 2C: Plan

**BrandScript source:** Plan

This section removes fear and confusion from the buying process. It shows how easy it is to work with you.

**Choose one plan type — never mix them:**

- **Pre-purchase plan** (how to get started): Everything leading up to the sale. Make it easy to take the first step.
  > *1. Book a call on a date that works.*
  > *2. Tell us your challenges and goals.*
  > *3. Get a proposal within 24 hours.*

- **Post-purchase plan** (your secret framework): What happens after they buy. Highlights your unique methodology and positions you as the expert.
  > *1. Take our 6-part assessment.*
  > *2. Get a custom program built for you.*
  > *3. Attend weekly check-ins for maximum results.*

**When in doubt, use pre-purchase.** It's easier to create and easier for customers to understand. Post-purchase plans tempt you into jargon and too many steps.

**Rules:**
- **Exactly 3 steps.** The brain struggles to hold more than three things at once. Phone numbers are split into groups of 3-4 for this reason. If reality has 20 steps, group them into 3 phases.
- **Name the plan.** A named plan has dramatically higher perceived value: "The Easy Installation Plan," "The Customer Satisfaction Agreement."
- **Never combine pre-purchase and post-purchase** into one sequence (e.g., from call-to-action all the way to success). It doesn't work.
- **Consider adding a guarantee** near the plan: "Satisfaction guaranteed," "Your money back," "3-year warranty." These alleviate trust obstacles for people buying from someone on the internet.

#### Agreement Plan (optional, alongside the process plan)

Separate from the process plan, an **agreement plan** is a list of promises that address the customer's specific fears about doing business with you. CarMax's agreement: *no haggling, no commission salespeople, every car certified.* These often live behind the scenes — on a "why us" section or near the plan — rather than above the fold. If the customer has known fears (being lied to, hidden fees, poor quality), an agreement plan directly neutralizes them.

## Section 3: FOMO

**BrandScript source:** Failure + Transitional Call to Action

### 3A: Failure (the stakes)

A short, pointed message about the cost of inaction. If there's no cost to not hiring you, there's no reason to hire you.

This is the "pinch of salt" — just enough to twist the knife. Too much fear drives people away; too little and they don't act.

**The Fear Appeal (4 steps)** — a soft, ethical structure for stakes copy:

1. **Establish vulnerability.** *"Nearly 30% of homes have termite damage."*
2. **Establish that action reduces it.** *"You should do something about it."*
3. **Offer a specific protective action.** *"We offer a complete home treatment."*
4. **Challenge them to take it.** *"Schedule today."*

Frame it around **what they lose**, not what they gain:
- Lost money, lost time, ongoing frustration
- The problem getting worse
- Competitors pulling ahead

### 3B: Transitional Call to Action (lead magnet)

Not everyone who visits will be ready to buy. If they leave and never come back, you've lost them. Offer something irresistible in exchange for a way to stay in touch (usually an email address).

Effective formats:
- Free PDF / downloadable guide
- Free assessment or quiz
- Video series or webinar
- Free trial or demo
- Checklist or template

The transitional CTA should:
1. **Stake a claim to your territory** — position you as the expert
2. **Create reciprocity** — giving freely makes people want to give back
3. **Keep the relationship alive** — you now have a way to follow up

## The One-Liner

The BrandScript should produce a **one-liner** — a single statement using four of the seven elements:

**Formula:** *"We help [character] who struggle with [problem] by [plan] so that they [success]."*

Examples:
- *"We help busy moms get a short, meaningful workout so they stay healthy and have renewed energy."*
- *"We save retirees the cost of a second home in Florida while delivering the warm beaches and luxury they love."*

Use the one-liner as:
- The clarifying sentence under the hero headline
- The site's `<meta name="description">` tag
- Email signatures, social bios, and anywhere the brand needs a verbal calling card

---

## Multiple Revenue Streams

If the business has multiple products or divisions, don't list them all equally above the fold. Find an **umbrella message** that unifies them, then split into clear sub-paths below.

Example: *"The key to success is a customized plan"* → buttons for "Personal" vs. "Corporate."

Each sub-path can have its own sub-BrandScript and landing page, but the homepage speaks to the shared core desire.

## Principles That Apply Everywhere

These principles govern the entire website, not just one section.

### Write in Morse code

Aim to cut your text in half, then cut it again. Some of the best websites use under ten sentences total. People scan — they don't read. Every word must earn its place. If a sentence doesn't serve one of the seven BrandScript elements, it's noise. Cut it.

### Clarity beats cleverness

> *"I'm a plumber. It's affordable. We fix things fast."*
> vs.
> *"Turning your pipe dreams into reality."*

The clever version makes the writer feel smart but confuses the customer. Clarity lowers cognitive load. People's brains aren't looking to interpret wordplay — they want to know what you do and what to do next.

### One CTA, repeated often

The direct CTA appears in:
- The navigation bar (top-right, contrasting color)
- The hero section (center, above the fold)
- After the 3Ps section
- In or after the FOMO section
- Anywhere the user might be ready to act

Same wording. Same color. People aren't annoyed by a repeated button — they're relieved to know what to do next.

### Every page has one job

Don't ask for too much at once. The contact page drives contact — not blog reading. The services page drives the CTA — not newsletter signups. When everything is important, nothing is important.

### The Rule of Three

The brain holds three things comfortably. Apply this everywhere:
- 3 benefits, not 5
- 3 services highlighted, not 7
- 3 steps in the plan, not 6
- 3 testimonials, not 12

Every item beyond three steals clarity.

### Speed matters

- A fast-loading site signals professionalism. A loading animation signals "leave."
- Amazon proved that even 1 second of delay costs billions. You can't afford a slow site either.
- Optimize images, use fast hosting, minimize bloat.

### Loss aversion over gain framing

People are 2-3x more motivated to avoid a loss than to achieve an equivalent gain. Frame copy around what they'll lose by not acting, not just what they'll gain by acting.

### Before/after over abstract promises

Show the transformation visually when possible. Before-and-after photos beat spreadsheets. "Life with us vs. life without us" comparisons beat vague claims.

### Consistency across channels

If the website says one thing, social media says another, and the sales script says something else, trust breaks down. The homepage headline should echo in the Instagram bio, email signature, and elevator pitch. A consistent story across all touchpoints multiplies StoryBrand's clarity.

### Systems over rock stars

The website should do the heavy lifting — not one salesperson or the founder:
- **Automated scheduling** — if your CTA is "book a call," let them book instantly. No "we'll call you back."
- **Speed to lead** — when someone fills out a form, they get an instant confirmation email. Ideally a personal reach-out before the actual meeting.
- **Instant follow-up** on every lead capture.

### Iterate, don't perfect

Launch, measure, adjust, repeat. Don't wait for the perfect site. Don't change the headline every day either. Change it, watch how conversions react, then tweak. Continuous improvement keeps your message sharp as the market shifts.

## Implementation Checklist

When building the website, verify each item:

### Hero Section
- [ ] Philosophical statement (or aspirational identity) as headline
- [ ] Clarifying sentence (one-liner or plain description of what the business does)
- [ ] Direct CTA button — brightest element on screen
- [ ] Optional transitional CTA (less prominent)
- [ ] Passes the grunt test (offer / benefit / next step in 5 seconds)
- [ ] Hero doesn't fill the entire viewport — hints at next section
- [ ] Success imagery (happy people experiencing the outcome)
- [ ] Clean navigation with CTA in top-right corner

### Promise Section
- [ ] 3-4 yes-questions (or emotional problem paragraph)
- [ ] Bridges from pain to solution
- [ ] Stays in emotional/philosophical territory, not technical
- [ ] Uses loss aversion framing where appropriate
- [ ] Success mapped to status, completeness, and/or self-realization

### Proof Section
- [ ] Brief empathetic guide introduction (origin story)
- [ ] 3 stories of transformation (testimonials with story arc)
- [ ] Video testimonials if available
- [ ] Logos, certifications, awards where appropriate

### Plan Section
- [ ] Exactly 3 steps
- [ ] Pre-purchase OR post-purchase (never both combined)
- [ ] Plan is named
- [ ] Optional agreement plan addressing specific customer fears
- [ ] Optional guarantee or trust-building claim
- [ ] No jargon — plain language throughout

### FOMO Section
- [ ] Short failure message using the fear appeal (vulnerability → action → protection → challenge)
- [ ] Loss-framed, not gain-framed
- [ ] Transitional CTA / lead magnet with clear value exchange
- [ ] Email capture mechanism

### Global
- [ ] One-liner used as meta description and/or clarifying sentence
- [ ] One CTA repeated throughout (nav, hero, after 3Ps, after FOMO)
- [ ] Rule of three applied everywhere (benefits, services, steps, testimonials)
- [ ] Morse code writing — cut text in half, every word earns its place
- [ ] Clarity over cleverness in all copy
- [ ] Fast page load (optimized images, minimal bloat)
- [ ] Each page has exactly one job
- [ ] Multiple revenue streams unified under one umbrella message (if applicable)
- [ ] Copy consistent with other channels (social, email, sales)
- [ ] Automated scheduling and instant follow-up systems in place
